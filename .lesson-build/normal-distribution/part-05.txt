<!-- EDIT: Common mistakes -->
<section id="mistakes" aria-labelledby="mistakes-h">
  <h2 id="mistakes-h">Common mistakes and exam traps</h2>

  <div class="mistake">
    <h4>1. Assuming everything is normal <span class="tag">Assumption error</span></h4>
    <p><strong>Why it&rsquo;s wrong:</strong> many datasets (incomes, waiting times, defect counts) are skewed,
    so normal-based percentages will be badly off.
    <strong>How to recognise it:</strong> a lopsided histogram, or a mean far from the median.
    <strong>How to avoid it:</strong> always plot the data before applying the empirical rule or z-scores.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="mistake">
    <h4>2. Mixing up standard deviation and variance <span class="tag">Calculation error</span></h4>
    <p><strong>Why it&rsquo;s wrong:</strong> variance is &sigma;&sup2; &mdash; the standard deviation squared. Dividing
    by variance instead of &sigma; gives a meaningless z-score.
    <strong>How to recognise it:</strong> z-scores that look absurdly small (e.g. 0.03 for an obviously extreme value).
    <strong>How to avoid it:</strong> check units &mdash; &sigma; is in the same units as the data; variance is in squared units.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="mistake">
    <h4>3. Reading &ldquo;below&rdquo; when the question asks &ldquo;above&rdquo; <span class="tag">Interpretation error</span></h4>
    <p><strong>Why it&rsquo;s wrong:</strong> tables and software usually give the area <em>below</em> a value.
    If the question asks for the proportion above, you must subtract from 1 (or 100%).
    <strong>How to recognise it:</strong> an answer like &ldquo;97.7% of bottles are overfilled&rdquo; when overfilling should be rare.
    <strong>How to avoid it:</strong> sketch the curve and shade the region you actually want before calculating.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="mistake">
    <h4>4. Dropping the minus sign <span class="tag">Calculation error</span></h4>
    <p><strong>Why it&rsquo;s wrong:</strong> z = &minus;1.5 and z = +1.5 are equally &ldquo;unusual&rdquo; but on opposite
    sides of the mean &mdash; one is below average, one above.
    <strong>How to recognise it:</strong> conclusions that don&rsquo;t match common sense (&ldquo;the shortest person is above average height&rdquo;).
    <strong>How to avoid it:</strong> always compute x &minus; &mu; carefully and keep the sign through the whole calculation.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="mistake">
    <h4>5. Treating 68&ndash;95&ndash;99.7 as exact for any dataset <span class="tag">Exam trap</span></h4>
    <p><strong>Why it&rsquo;s wrong:</strong> the rule holds only for (approximately) normal data, and the numbers
    themselves are rounded (the true values are 68.27%, 95.45%, 99.73%).
    <strong>How to recognise it:</strong> exam questions that sneak in a skewed dataset and expect you to notice.
    <strong>How to avoid it:</strong> state the normality assumption whenever you use the rule &mdash; examiners often award marks for it.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="mistake">
    <h4>6. Using the density instead of the cumulative probability in Excel <span class="tag">Software-output error</span></h4>
    <p><strong>Why it&rsquo;s wrong:</strong> <code>NORM.DIST(x, mean, sd, FALSE)</code> returns the curve&rsquo;s
    <em>height</em>, not a probability. Only the cumulative form (<code>TRUE</code>) gives &ldquo;% of values below x.&rdquo;
    <strong>How to recognise it:</strong> &ldquo;probabilities&rdquo; that don&rsquo;t land between sensible bounds, or results that change wildly with units.
    <strong>How to avoid it:</strong> for proportions, always set the last argument of <code>NORM.DIST</code> to <code>TRUE</code>.</p>
  </div>
</section>

  <h3>Minitab</h3>

  <h2 id="practice-h">Practice activity: the bottling line</h2>
  <div class="panel">
    <p><strong>Scenario:</strong> a bottling machine fills juice bottles with a mean of
    <strong>&mu; = 350&nbsp;ml</strong> and a standard deviation of <strong>&sigma; = 10&nbsp;ml</strong>.
    Historical fill volumes are approximately normal.</p>
    <p><strong>Your task:</strong> using only the empirical rule (no calculator needed), answer:</p>
    <ol>
      <li>What percentage of bottles contain between <strong>340&nbsp;ml and 360&nbsp;ml</strong>?</li>
      <li>What percentage of bottles contain <strong>more than 370&nbsp;ml</strong>?</li>
      <li>The legal minimum on the label is <strong>320&nbsp;ml</strong>. Roughly how often should a bottle fall below it?</li>
    </ol>
    <p>Work each one out (a quick sketch of the curve helps), then check yourself.</p>

      <div class="reveal-body" id="practice-answer" hidden>
        <p><strong>1. About 68%.</strong> 340 and 360 are exactly &mu; &minus; 1&sigma; and &mu; + 1&sigma;
        (350 &minus; 10 and 350 + 10). The empirical rule says about 68% of values fall within &plusmn;1&sigma;.</p>
        <p><strong>2. About 2.
