How to Use the Steel Phase & Transformation Explorer

Move from chemistry and processing conditions to predicted microstructure, properties, and performance. The explorer combines equilibrium and kinetic transformation analysis, applied heat-treatment models, thermal-data analysis, metallurgy laboratories, the exact supplied rapid-quench reference, and the complete Buehler iron-carbon poster with calibrated interactive overlays.

Before you begin

Purpose and limits

The explorer is a teaching and preliminary engineering-screening environment organized around:

Chemistry → Processing → Microstructure → Properties → Performance

Best uses

Learning metallurgy, comparing scenarios, identifying fields, visualizing heat treatment, screening thermal records, troubleshooting measurements, and developing hypotheses.

Not a replacement for

Controlled standards, certified grade data, validated thermodynamic or kinetic software, calibrated measurements, laboratory testing, procedure qualification, material disposition, or engineering approval.

Engineering caution: calculations and interactive classifications are educational models. Never use the tool alone to release material, qualify a process, establish hazardous operating conditions, or approve a product.

Interpretation basis

Use Equilibrium for Fe–Fe3C phase fields and lever-rule fractions. Use Rapid cooling for generalized ferrite, pearlite, bainite, martensite, and retained-austenite tendencies.

Critical-temperature terminology

  • Ae: equilibrium critical temperature.
  • Ac: transformation observed during heating.
  • Ar: transformation observed during cooling.
  • Ms/Mf: martensite start and finish concepts.

Core modules

Equilibrium, thermal paths, TTT/CCT, and chemistry

Equilibrium diagram

  1. Select metric or imperial display.
  2. Enter carbon and temperature.
  3. Use + Add point for comparisons.
  4. Select a row or marker to activate a point.
  5. Click or drag the diagram to reposition it.

Use tie lines, spotlighting, critical-boundary snapping, connected paths, lever-rule fractions, and the schematic microstructure viewer.

fα = (Cγ − C₀)/(Cγ − Cα)    and    fγ = (C₀ − Cα)/(Cγ − Cα)

Heating and cooling paths

Select a preset or build a custom sequence of heating, holding, cooling, quenching, and tempering. Use Play, Pause, or the time slider, then review boundary crossings and the live schematic.

TTT and CCT

TTT

Use isothermal temperature and hold time to examine generalized transformation-start and finish behavior.

CCT

Use cooling rate and final temperature to compare generalized ferrite, pearlite, bainite, martensite, and retained-austenite tendencies.

Chemistry and causal chain

Enter verified chemistry to screen CE IIW, Pcm, Ac1, Ac3, Ms, hardenability, weldability, and directional property trends. The causal dashboard connects chemistry, processing, microstructure, properties, and performance.

Release 2

Applied heat-treatment modules

Jominy and section-size hardenability

Select geometry, section size, quench medium, agitation, prior-austenite grain number, and target hardness. Read the estimated Jominy curve, equivalent Jominy distance, martensite fraction, and surface-to-centre hardness profile.

Austenitization and grain growth

Set temperature, hold time, section size, heating rate, starting microstructure, carbide burden, pinning, and initial grain size. Balance heat-through, dissolution, homogenization, and grain growth.

Quench-medium comparison

Compare air/gas, oils, polymer, water, brine, and the salt-bath analogue under common assumptions. Review cooling, martensite, hardness, thermal gradients, cracking risk, and distortion risk.

Safety: the tool does not provide hazardous quench, hot-oil, brine, polymer, molten-salt, or high-temperature operating instructions.

Release 3

Process-data analysis and temperature measurement

  1. Open Process Data.
  2. Select a CSV, TSV, TXT, or semicolon-delimited file.
  3. Map time and temperature columns.
  4. Select source units.
  5. Adjust smoothing and arrest sensitivity.
Cooling rate = −dT/dt

Review cooling-rate windows, candidate arrests, proximity to critical temperatures, measured-versus-simulated paths, and RMSE. Candidate arrests are screening signals—not confirmed transformations.

Measurement assistant

Use thermocouple mode for type, diameter, attachment, shielding, thermal mass, and response-time risks. Use pyrometer mode for emissivity, reflected temperature, target-to-spot ratio, and surface-condition errors.

Surface and diffusion

Model carburizing, carbonitriding, nitriding, ferritic nitrocarburizing, induction, flame, and laser-hardening trends.

Tempering

Explore hardness loss, toughness recovery, stress relief, retained austenite, secondary hardening, and embrittlement.

Mechanical tests

Use stress–strain, Charpy/DBTT, fatigue S–N, and hardness-conversion learning views.

Virtual metallography

Generate schematic ferrite, pearlite, bainite, martensite, spheroidite, decarburization, banding, and inclusions.

Solidification

Animate dendrites, segregation, porosity, rolling elongation, banding, and anisotropy.

Alloy families

Use family-specific indicators for plain/low-alloy, stainless, tool steel, and cast iron.

Release 5

Exact-source interactive reference diagrams

The Interactive Diagrams tab keeps the source visuals intact and places a calibrated interaction layer above them. It does not substitute a generic dashboard chart for the references.

Rapid-quench exact reference

The exact user-supplied 850 × 890 image is assembled locally and displayed as the visual base. Its title, graph proportions, °C/°F axes, grid, colors, curves, callouts, labels, and source texture remain visible without being redrawn.

Buehler iron-carbon poster

The complete Buehler poster is displayed at its original portrait proportions with its phase fields, heat-color column, crystal structures, micrographs, axes, logos, and source information. The explorer adds non-destructive overlays above it.

Source method: the rapid visual is the supplied image, not a reconstructed approximation. The interaction layer is calibrated to x = 80–701 pixels for 0–1.2 wt% C and y = 90–792 pixels for 1000 to −100 °C.

Publication-rights caution: source ownership and reproduction permission remain separate from technical implementation. Confirm the right to publish the supplied rapid chart and the Buehler poster before production release.

The rapid-quench reference is a carbon–temperature teaching map, not a time-resolved TTT or CCT diagram. Interactive classifications must be verified against validated grade-specific data.

Accuracy and rights controls

Engineering and publication cautions

  • Separate equilibrium phases from kinetic transformation products.
  • Bainite and martensite are not equilibrium fields on the Fe–Fe3C diagram.
  • Pearlite and bainite are microconstituents, not single phases.
  • Alloying, grain size, prior condition, section size, and cooling history shift real behavior.
  • Mechanical properties depend on morphology, texture, inclusions, precipitation, sampling, and test method.
  • Candidate thermal arrests and measurement corrections require independent verification.
  • Do not derive hazardous process settings from this educational tool.
  • Confirm public-display and reproduction rights before production release.

Before relying on an output

Final checklist

  • Confirm carbon and alloy chemistry.
  • Confirm temperature units.
  • Select the correct diagram and interpretation basis.
  • Separate equilibrium phases from kinetic products.
  • Document thermal path, time, and section size.
  • Verify grade-specific TTT/CCT or thermodynamic data.
  • Treat interactive classifications as educational estimates.
  • Confirm the source image is loaded and the point lies inside the calibrated graph rectangle.
  • Confirm publication rights before public release.
  • Verify with metallography, hardness, chemistry, or mechanical testing.
  • Obtain technical approval where required.
